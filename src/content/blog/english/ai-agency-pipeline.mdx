## The Workflows

Here's what's actually running:

### 1. Lead Research & Enrichment

When a new lead enters the system, an n8n workflow fires automatically. It pulls business information, validates the data, scores the lead against my target niches â€” dentists, trades, beauticians, wedding boutiques â€” and populates the CRM with everything it finds. What used to take 20 minutes of manual research per lead now takes a few seconds, and the data is structured consistently every time.

### 2. Cold Outreach Pipeline

This is the biggest piece. Seven workflows handle the full outreach sequence: sourcing leads, generating personalised email copy via Ollama, dispatching through Zoho, tracking opens and replies, and queuing follow-ups based on interaction data pulled from the CRM API.

The email copy isn't generic â€” Ollama pulls context about the lead's niche, their likely pain points, and which case study is most relevant before it writes anything. A dentist gets a different email to a construction firm, and both feel written by a human who did their homework.

The dispatch controller runs on a schedule. I don't touch it day-to-day.

### 3. Follow-Up Sequencing

Following up is where most solo operators fall down. Not because they forget â€” because remembering to follow up on 40 leads at exactly the right interval is genuinely hard to do manually.

The follow-up workflow checks the CRM daily for any leads where a follow-up is due, pulls the interaction history, and either sends the next email in the sequence or flags the lead for manual review if something unusual has happened â€” like a reply that needs a human response.

Blog posts, LinkedIn drafts, and a client newsletter all run through a content pipeline. A brief goes in â€” topic, target audience, key points â€” Ollama drafts the content, and it lands in a review queue. For the blog specifically, approved posts are submitted as a GitHub pull request against the site repo. I merge when I'm happy, and Netlify deploys automatically. The whole thing from brief to live post can happen in under an hour when I'm not precious about it.

## The Human Checkpoints

This is the part people skip over when they talk about AI automation, and it's probably the most important bit.

Every workflow that touches a customer has a human gate.

Email copy gets reviewed before the dispatch controller goes live. Blog posts go through a pull request â€” I merge when I'm happy with them. Newsletter drafts land in a review queue for approval. Leads that respond to outreach get flagged for me to handle personally.

AI is fast and cheap at generating output. It's also confident when it's wrong. The checkpoints aren't a workaround for AI being bad â€” they're the design. The system makes my decision-making faster and better-informed. It doesn't replace my judgement.

The other thing AI is genuinely useful for that doesn't get talked about enough: **ideation and working through problems**. When I'm trying to figure out how to approach a new niche, structure a campaign, or think through a data model, having a model to think out loud with is genuinely valuable. It'll surface angles I hadn't considered, push back on assumptions, and help me stress-test an approach before I build it. That's not a shortcut â€” it's a thinking tool.

## Choosing the Right Model for the Job

Not all LLMs are equal, and this matters more than most people realise. Running everything through one model is like using the same tool for every job in a workshop.

My current split:

- **Qwen3:8b** â€” code tasks, data transformation, anything structural
- **Mistral-nemo** â€” email copy, blog drafts, anything that needs to read like a human wrote it
- **Llama3.1:8b** â€” general reasoning, classification, research summaries

Getting this wrong produces obvious results. Early versions of the outreach emails, generated with the wrong model, read exactly like AI-generated content. Switching models made a bigger difference than any amount of prompt engineering.

## Technical Implementation

*Not interested in the stack? [Skip to the next section.](#whats-next)*

The local AI setup uses Ollama running on a MacBook Air (M-series), exposed over a private [Tailscale](https://tailscale.com) mesh network so the VPS running n8n can reach it without opening any public ports. The models run entirely on-device â€” no data leaves the machine, and inference costs nothing.

The CRM is a custom Laravel application on the same VPS as n8n, with a Bearer-authenticated REST API that all the workflows talk to. Leads, interactions, follow-up schedules, content drafts â€” it all lives there, queryable and writable by any workflow that needs it.

The website (this one) is built with Astro, hosted on Netlify. Blog content is markdown files in a GitHub repo â€” pushing a PR is how content moves from the n8n pipeline to the live site.
