## Why This Matters More Than You Think

### 1. You're Excluding 20% of Your Market

One in five UK adults lives with a disability. That's 14.6 million people—and they control an estimated **£274 billion in spending power** (the "Purple Pound").

When your website isn't accessible:
- Screen readers can't navigate your content
- Keyboard-only users can't fill in your contact forms
- People with visual impairments can't read low-contrast text
- Users with motor difficulties struggle with tiny clickable areas

These aren't edge cases. These are potential clients who need legal services and are willing to pay for them—if they can actually use your website.

### 2. You're Breaching the Equality Act 2010

Under the Equality Act 2010, service providers must make "reasonable adjustments" to ensure disabled people can access their services. Your website is a service.

Recent case law has established that inaccessible websites can constitute discrimination. While enforcement has been limited in the UK compared to the US, the legal precedent exists—and you don't want to be the test case.

## What Does "Failing Accessibility" Actually Mean?

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.2 set the international standard. At Level AA (the standard most public sector sites must meet), common failures include:

- **Missing alt text on images** — screen readers can't describe visual content
- **Insufficient colour contrast** — text becomes unreadable for people with low vision
- **No keyboard navigation** — many users can't use a mouse
- **Poor form labels** — screen readers can't identify what each field is for
- **Inaccessible PDFs** — documents can't be parsed by assistive technology
- **Missing skip links** — keyboard users have to tab through entire navigation on every page

Most of these issues are straightforward to fix—**if you know they exist**.
